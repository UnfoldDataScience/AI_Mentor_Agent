# How to Learn AI and Machine Learning Effectively

## Build before you fully understand

A common trap is trying to master all the theory before writing any code.
Instead, build small projects early — even if you don't understand every
detail yet. Seeing a model actually train, predict, or generate text makes the
underlying math far easier to absorb later, because you now have a concrete
picture to attach the theory to.

## Learn in a spiral, not a straight line

AI/ML topics are deeply interconnected — you can't fully understand neural
networks without some linear algebra, and you can't fully understand
transformers without some neural network basics. Rather than trying to learn
everything about one topic before moving to the next, go through topics in
layers: get a working understanding of the basics across several areas first,
then revisit each one in more depth. Each pass deepens the previous one.

## The 70/20/10 time split

A useful rule of thumb for allocating study time:
- **70%** hands-on practice — coding, building projects, working through
  exercises
- **20%** reading and watching structured material — courses, documentation,
  papers
- **10%** community — discussions, code reviews, asking and answering
  questions

Most self-learners over-invest in the 20% (endless tutorials) and under-invest
in the 70% (actually building things), which leads to "tutorial fatigue"
without real skill growth.

## Pick one project that scares you a little

Progress accelerates when you have a slightly-too-ambitious project as your
anchor. It forces you to learn things in the order a real problem demands,
rather than the order a course presents them — which is usually closer to how
skills are used on the job.

## Track your learning, not just your time

Time spent studying is a poor signal of progress. Instead, keep a short log of
**what you can now do that you couldn't do last week** — a new technique
applied, a bug you debugged, a concept you explained to someone else. This
keeps motivation grounded in real capability growth, especially during the
weeks where progress feels invisible.

## When you're stuck

- Step away and explain the problem out loud or in writing — often the act of
  explaining reveals the gap.
- Reduce the problem to the smallest version that still reproduces the issue.
- It's normal for debugging and "why doesn't this work" to take longer than
  writing the original code — budget time for it accordingly.
